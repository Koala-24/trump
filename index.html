The TRUMP EFFECT

The impact of our southerly neighbour’s new security measures on business travel

by Laura Bickle Given our close ties to the US—geographically, economically and culturally—it’s no surprise that its recent policy changes have ramifications here in Canada—and on the corporate meeting, event, travel and incentive industries.

Source: The Global Business Travel Association (GBTA) survey conducted March 7-8, 2017 In the days after the first US travel ban was announced in January, Anita Carlyle CMP, CMM, managing partner at Moore Carlyle Consulting and MCC Destination Management, reports that three of their programs changed as a direct result of the US action. One of them, an Italian incentive group that had planned to visit Chicago and New York, changed their destinations to Toronto and Montreal. “They didn’t want a possible hassle at the US border.” She also says that some US groups are hesitant to travel abroad because of green card issues and negative sentiment toward the US abroad.

At press time, a revised travel ban—which was to go into effect March 16 and prevents foreign nationals from Sudan, Syria, Iran, Libya, Somalia and Yemen who don’t have a valid visa from entering the United States for 90 days—was held up in the courts, but it’s clear this is an issue we will have to grapple with for some time. A NEW WRINKLE

On March 21, US-bound travellers from 10 airports in Turkey, the Middle East and North Africa were banned from carrying large electronic devices on board, including tablets, laptops and Kindles. The Department of Homeland Security cited fears that bombs can be concealed in such devices. The ACTE responded on the same day: “Does the Department of Homeland Security know about a threat so great that it can’t be shared with the business travel industry? Or are these latest restrictions about to resonate as the new norm the world over? Speculation rivals uncertainty for bad news in the travel industry. Answers are needed now,” said executive director Greeley Koch, adding: “The first rule in business travel is not to be separated from anything essential to the success of your trip. And the most important component is your laptop or tablet. Travellers who do not check baggage normally, will now have to check their laptops, tablets and e-readers on the affected flights. Baggage goes missing every day. Can you imagine the consequences of losing a week’s, or a month’s work, plus your confidential corporate data to a luggage theft?”
